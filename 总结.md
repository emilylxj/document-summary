Integrated Modeling of Tokamak Plasma Dynamics: Coupling Equilibrium and Transport Solvers

I. Introduction and Foundational Principles

A. Context and Motivation: The Role of Integrated Modeling in Fusion Energy Development

The successful realization of magnetic confinement fusion hinges on the capability to accurately predict and actively control the complex, nonlinear dynamics of the tokamak plasma. Plasma equilibrium is universally acknowledged as a fundamental and essential element influencing all other physical phenomena, including magneto-hydrodynamic (MHD) instabilities, plasma flows and waves, and kinetic transport and turbulence.[1] Integrated Modeling (IM) provides the critical framework necessary to simulate the simultaneous evolution of the macroscopic magnetic geometry and the microscopic plasma profiles (density, temperature, current).

This field addresses a significant multiscale challenge: plasma dynamics span timescales from the rapid kinetic effects of turbulence (microseconds) up to the slow resistive current diffusion (tens to hundreds of seconds). Integrated modeling achieves predictive capability by solving the dominant slow-scale processes—primarily plasma transport—while providing physics closures that account for the effects of fast physics (turbulence) and maintaining a consistent magnetic geometry throughout the simulation duration.

The requirement for coupled solvers is mandatory because the system is deeply interdependent. The magnetic geometry, defined by the poloidal flux ψ, determines the flux-surface shaping and metric coefficients, which are essential inputs for calculating transport coefficients. Conversely, the evolving pressure and current profiles (outputs of the transport step) serve as the source terms required to recalculate the magnetic equilibrium via the Grad-Shafranov (GS) equation.

B. The Tokamak Equilibrium Problem: The Grad-Shafranov (GS) Equation

The Grad-Shafranov equation is the elliptic partial differential equation (PDE) that describes ideal magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) equilibrium in two-dimensional, axisymmetric toroidal geometry. It is foundational for defining the magnetic flux surfaces in a tokamak.[2] The GS equation in cylindrical coordinates (R,Z) is typically written as:

−μ0​1​(R∂R∂​(R1​∂R∂ψ​)+∂Z2∂2ψ​)=Jϕ​(R,Z)

where ψ is the poloidal magnetic flux function, μ0​ is the permeability of free space, and Jϕ​(R,Z) is the toroidal current density.

The intrinsic difficulty arises because the source term, Jϕ​, is highly nonlinear. In equilibrium, Jϕ​ is determined by the plasma pressure P(ψ) and the poloidal current function F(ψ), where F=RBϕ​ (toroidal magnetic field component):

Jϕ​=RdψdP​+μ0​R1​FdψdF​

**Free-Boundary Solvers (FBS):** While earlier studies often assumed a known or fixed plasma boundary, modern advanced studies, exemplified by the "Development of a free boundary Tokamak Equilibrium Solver (TES)" [1, 3], focus on the free-boundary problem. In this scenario, the plasma boundary is determined dynamically by the magnetic fields generated by the plasma current and the external coils.[1] Free-boundary solutions are vital for accessing equilibria with new topological features, such as snow-flake (SF) or X-divertor configurations.[1]

C. The Transport Problem: Governing Equations and Characteristic Timescales

Transport modeling in tokamaks generally adopts a 1.5-dimensional (1.5D) approach. This method assumes that transport is much faster along the magnetic field lines than across them, allowing plasma profiles to be considered constant on a given flux surface. The plasma dynamics are then reduced to a set of parabolic PDEs describing the evolution of flux-surface-averaged quantities along a normalized radial coordinate (ρ or ψN​).

The core evolution equations govern:

1. **Particle Density (**ne​,ni​**):** Evolution driven by particle sources, diffusion, and convection.

2. **Energy (Temperature** Te​,Ti​**):** Evolution driven by auxiliary heating, fusion power, Ohmic dissipation, radiative losses, and diffusive/convective heat fluxes.

3. **Poloidal Magnetic Flux (**ψp​**):** Governed by the Current Diffusion Equation (CDE), which describes the relaxation of the current profile due to resistivity.

These transport equations are fundamentally coupled with the electromagnetic equations to describe the time evolution of the plasma.[4] The results—evolving density and temperature profiles—determine P(ψ) and the electrical resistivity η, which in turn drives the evolution of the magnetic equilibrium via the CDE and GS equation.

II. Principle of Equilibrium and Transport Coupling

The integration of the GS equation and the transport equations is mandatory for predicting the time-evolution of a tokamak discharge. The coupling mechanism ensures that the magnetic geometry remains consistent with the plasma state profiles, and vice versa.

A. The Iterative Solution of the Grad-Shafranov Equation

Due to the inherent nonlinearity stemming from the dependence of Jϕ​ on ψ, the GS equation must be solved iteratively. The Picard iteration scheme is a standard method employed by equilibrium solvers.[1]

In the n-th iteration, the toroidal current density Jϕ(n)​ is calculated based on the magnetic geometry ψ(n−1) determined from the previous step. The GS equation is then solved for ψ(n). For free-boundary problems, the current density formulation is often expressed using a normalized poloidal flux ψs​≡(ψ−ψa​)/(ψb​−ψa​), ensuring that the plasma current density Jϕ,pl​(R,Z) automatically diminishes to zero at the plasma boundary.[1] The input profiles for pressure P(ψ) and the poloidal current term F(ψ) are dynamically derived from the temperature and density profiles calculated during the transport step.[1]

B. The Current Diffusion Equation (CDE) and Flux Evolution

The CDE is the essential kinematic link between the thermal/particle transport equations and the GS equation. It governs the evolution of the toroidal current density Jϕ​ (and thus the safety factor q profile) over the resistive timescale, which is typically slower than the energy confinement time.

The CDE predicts the change in the poloidal magnetic flux ψp​ based on the plasma resistivity η (derived from Te​) and the imposed/self-generated electric fields. The resulting Jϕ​ profile, which evolves due to diffusion and current drive sources, becomes the non-homogeneous source term required by the elliptic GS solver.[4]

C. Integrated Coupling Strategies: Predictive vs. Reconstruction

Tokamak simulation codes integrate the GS and transport solutions through two primary strategies, depending on the application context:

**1. Predictive Coupling:** This approach is used primarily for scenario design, operational planning (e.g., for ITER), and control design. The system predicts the evolution of n,T,Jϕ​ profiles over time based solely on actuator inputs and physics models.[4]

**2. Inverse/Reconstruction Coupling:** This strategy, exemplified by its implementation at ASDEX Upgrade, couples the predictive Current Diffusion Equation with an inverse Grad-Shafranov equilibrium solver (like IDE) that minimizes a least-squares criterion based on measured and modeled data.[2, 5]

This method is crucial for offline, routine equilibrium analysis shortly after a discharge.[5] When internal measurements of the current distribution are sparse or noisy, the equilibrium reconstruction becomes ill-posed in the plasma core. The predictive CDE model compensates for these diagnostic deficiencies by providing a sensible estimation of the current distribution evolution between successive measurements, thereby stabilizing and improving the accuracy of the reconstruction.[5]

D. The Coupled Predictor-Corrector Time Stepping Procedure

Integrated modeling codes manage computational efficiency by leveraging the disparity in timescales: the transport timescale is long relative to the GS solution time. The computational workflow is an iterative predictor-corrector cycle.

**Standard Procedure Cycle:** The most fundamental cycle involves sequentially advancing the plasma profiles (Step 1), calculating the current diffusion (Step 2), and then solving the GS equation (Step 3) to obtain the new, self-consistent equilibrium geometry at time t+Δt.[4]

**Acceleration Strategy (Decoupling):** A robust strategy for acceleration involves running multiple transport time steps before evaluating and updating the full equilibrium configuration and geometric factors.[6] This modified procedure significantly improves speed; for cases with constant plasma cross-section (fixed boundary), this modified process has been shown to run approximately twice as fast as continuously updating the equilibrium.[6]

However, the efficacy of decoupling the equilibrium update is highly dependent on the discharge phase. While efficient for flat-top or near-steady states where the geometry is stable, rapid changes in the equilibrium configuration (such as during the start-up phase, involving magnetic shaping, boundary changes, and an increase in the Shafranov shift) critically impact the geometric factors and, consequently, the transport coefficients.[7] If the equilibrium calculation is skipped during such dynamic phases, the estimated geometric parameters used by the transport solver may rapidly diverge from the physical reality, compromising the dynamic accuracy.[7] Therefore, achieving agreement with physical results requires that high-frequency GS updates be maintained when geometry changes are large, even if this reduces the computational gain achieved in steady state.

III. Numerical Solution Methods for Transport Solvers

The accuracy, stability, and speed of integrated modeling rely heavily on the sophistication of the numerical schemes employed to solve the highly nonlinear and stiff set of transport equations.

A. Temporal Discretization Schemes

The time-dependent transport equations are inherently parabolic and exhibit extreme stiffness. This stiffness arises from the combination of large diffusive terms and the small characteristic lengths associated with turbulent transport models.

**Implicit Schemes:** Explicit time-stepping schemes are generally unsuitable because they are limited by severe stability criteria (CFL constraints), necessitating impractically small time steps for long-pulse simulations. Consequently, implicit or mixed implicit-explicit (IMEX) schemes are required.

**Implicit-Explicit (IMEX) Schemes:** These schemes enhance stability and efficiency by partitioning the equation: stiff terms (typically large diffusion terms) are treated implicitly (L2 formulation, high order), while non-stiff terms (often advective or source terms) are treated explicitly (L1 formulation, easy to solve).[8] A common strategy involves an iterative procedure, often inspired by the Picard–Lindelöf theorem, to converge the initially easy-to-solve IMEX solution toward the desired high-order, fully implicit solution.[8]

**Fully Implicit (FI) Schemes:** For simulating transient processes accurately and for robust, long-term evolution of stiff tokamak plasmas, Fully Implicit schemes are paramount.[9, 10] Recent developments in FI solvers demonstrate high temporal accuracy, often achieving second-order accuracy in time stepping, which dramatically improves the overall convergence characteristics compared to first-order schemes.[9] Solving the non-linear algebraic system resulting from FI discretization typically utilizes advanced methods such as the Newton-Krylov approach. To maintain high efficiency in large-scale simulations, parallelization and strategies based on spatial domain decomposition are often necessary for managing the large Jacobian matrices involved in the iteration.[10]

B. Spatial Discretization and Stabilization Techniques

The choice of spatial discretization (Finite Difference, Finite Volume, or Finite Element) impacts the method's ability to handle complex flux-surface geometry and gradients.

**Flux-Corrected Transport (FCT):** A fundamental necessity for transport equations that include strong advection or sharp gradients, particularly in density and flow dynamics. FCT algorithms were specifically developed to solve generalized continuity equations while ensuring that the finite-difference formulae maintain positivity for the convected quantity.[11, 12]

In fluid-like plasma problems, FCT is particularly valuable because it conserves flux and eliminates spurious numerical oscillations (undershoots or overshoots) that typically appear near sharp gradients or shocks.[12] By using a conservative formulation without introducing artificial viscosity, FCT can accurately represent steep density or temperature profiles, resulting in transitions that span only one or two cell thicknesses, which is the sharpest stable representation on an Eulerian grid.[11, 12] This technique is frequently employed in transport codes (e.g., LCPFCT) modeling particle and thermal diffusion.[13]

C. Computational Acceleration Techniques

The inherent computational burden of integrated modeling requires aggressive acceleration techniques to make real-time applications and large-scale optimization feasible.

**Multigrid Methods (MG):** These methods are designed to accelerate the convergence of iterative solvers by addressing errors across multiple frequency scales. The **Angular Multigrid (ANMG)** method, often combined with the Lyusternik-Wagner extrapolation technique, has been demonstrated as effective in complex transport problems, such as neutron transport, even under conditions of strong anisotropic scattering.[14]

**Mode Multigrid (MMG):** A more recent and novel acceleration technique utilizes Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to analyze the convergence history of the steady-state flow field.[15] Unlike traditional multigrid methods that involve coarsening the physical computational mesh, MMG projects the flow field solutions from physical space into a modal space. By truncating high-frequency modes and retaining only the first-order mode, the method simplifies the convergence process. This approach ingeniously avoids the complicated and grid-dependent steps of mesh coarsening, making it highly versatile for unstructured grids and parallel computing with minimal modification to the core flow solver.[15] Verification tests show that MMG dramatically reduces the number of iterative steps, yielding speedups of 3 to 6 times over the original method while maintaining computational accuracy.[15]

**Hybrid Architecture Necessity:** The requirement for integrated modeling to incorporate high-fidelity physics, such as quasi-linear transport models, introduces high computational stiffness. The complexity of these accurate models necessitates the use of advanced numerical strategies—specifically, high-order, robust Fully Implicit time integration schemes to ensure stability.[9] However, the resulting large, nonlinear algebraic systems require specialized iterative solvers (like Newton-Krylov) whose internal convergence must be managed and accelerated using techniques such as MMG or ANMG.[10, 15] Therefore, effective solver acceleration must simultaneously target both the stability of the time integration and the rate of convergence of the non-linear iterative steps.

B. Neoclassical and Turbulent Transport

Plasma transport is conventionally split into neoclassical (collision-dominated) and turbulent components.

**Neoclassical Transport:** This mechanism provides the baseline, irreducible transport, including viscosity, diffusion, and intrinsic non-inductive current drive. Specifically, neoclassical models are required to calculate the plasma resistivity (ηneo​) and the self-generated Bootstrap Current (Jbs​), both essential inputs for the Current Diffusion Equation used in equilibrium evolution.[13]

**Turbulent Transport Modeling:** The bulk of heat and particle loss in high-performance tokamaks is driven by micro-instabilities (e.g., Ion Temperature Gradient (ITG) modes or Trapped Electron Modes (TEM)). The turbulent transport coefficients (thermal diffusivities χi,e​ and particle convection Γi,e​) are fundamentally derived from complex, highly computational gyrokinetic models.[17, 18]

Due to the computational intensity, first-principles nonlinear gyrokinetic simulations are generally precluded from integrated transport simulations, which run over hundreds of seconds of plasma time.[17] Tractability is achieved by utilizing models based on the **quasi-linear (QL) approximation**, which are largely valid in the core of tokamak plasmas and, while validated against nonlinear results, provide a computational speedup of approximately six orders of magnitude compared to full nonlinear simulations.[17] QuaLiKiz is one example of a widely used quasi-linear gyrokinetic transport model.[17]

C. The Role of Neural Network (NN) Emulators in Acceleration

Despite the massive speedup gained by moving from nonlinear to quasi-linear models, QL solvers remain a bottleneck for high-frequency applications like real-time control or large-scale optimization. For instance, calculating the QuaLiKiz eigenvalue solution at a single radial point can take approximately one second.[17]

**NN Implementation:** This bottleneck is overcome by employing machine learning (ML) emulators. A Neural Network (NN) regression model (e.g., QLKNN) is trained on extensive databases compiled from running the original high-fidelity QL code.[17, 18]

The NN emulator is then coupled into the fast 1D core transport code, replacing the direct computation of the QL fluxes.[18] This strategy integrates the first-principle-based turbulent heat and particle transport coefficients (χi,e​ and Γi,e​) into the simulation workflow.[18] The resulting NN emulator is orders of magnitude faster than the original flux calculation, making it suitable for applications such as scenario development, trajectory optimization, and, crucially, real-time control applications.[17]

The implementation of this hybrid modeling environment establishes a necessary predictability hierarchy: the high computational cost of accurately modeling micro-turbulence is managed by outsourcing the flux calculation to a high-speed ML model. This ensures that the simulation maintains physical anchoring to first-principles physics while achieving the necessary speed for practical application in future devices like ITER, where fast pulse design simulators (PDS) are being developed.[18]

D. Handling Momentum Transport and Residual Stress

Momentum transport is vital for understanding plasma rotation and stability. The standard transport equations include an evolution equation for toroidal momentum.

Advanced gyrokinetic models, such as TGLF, provide the momentum flux output in the form of a stress tensor, which comprises both a standard diffusive (diagonal) part and a non-diffusive **residual stress** part.[16] If the residual stress term is calculated and provided, the transport code uses it in the momentum transport equation. However, caution must be exercised during the coupling process. To prevent physically inaccurate "double counting," the calculated diffusive part of the stress tensor must be explicitly subtracted before the residual component is incorporated into the momentum transport equation, especially if the transport solver already includes a standard model for momentum diffusion.[16]

The collective plasma pressure P, which acts as the source term P′(ψ) in the GS equation, results from the sum of the species pressures. While the lowest-order species pressure p0​ is assumed to be a flux-surface function, the full MHD equilibrium physics requires accounting for magnetic geometric corrections. This includes the Pfirsch-Schlüter poloidal pressure variation, p1​(ρ,θ), which reflects the first-order, toroidal-angle-averaged pressure variation on a flux surface caused by toroidal effects.[19]

B. Detailed Modeling of Source and Sink Terms

Accurate simulation demands detailed modeling of all sources and sinks, which themselves depend non-linearly on the plasma profiles (n,T, etc.).[4] The iterative nature of the solution must therefore continue until the solutions converge, satisfying the balance between transport fluxes, sources, and sinks.

1. **Particle Sources (**Sn​**):** Includes fueling mechanisms (e.g., gas puffing, pellet injection) and boundary interaction physics like recycling.

2. **Energy Sources (**Q**):** Crucial components include Ohmic heating, fusion power generated by thermal and energetic alpha particles, and auxiliary heating systems such as Neutral Beam Injection (NBI), Electron Cyclotron Heating (ECH), and Ion Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ICRH).

3. **Current Sources (**SJ​**):** These are essential for profile shaping and maintaining steady-state operation. They include externally driven currents (NBI-CD, ECCD, LHCD) and the internally driven bootstrap current (Jbs​).[13]

The total toroidal current density Jϕ​ driving the equilibrium solution must consistently account for the Flux-Surface Averaged (FSA) parallel current contribution and the Pfirsch-Schlüter current, ensuring the momentum balance is consistent with the MHD equilibrium constraints.[19]

C. Incorporating Time-Dependent Evolution and Geometry

Integrated simulations begin with a complete definition of the initial state, including plasma dimensions, cross-sectional shape, initial profiles (P,n,F), the initial equilibrium solution ψ(R,Z), and the safety factor q.[6]

As the plasma evolves over time, the 1.5D transport model depends critically on geometric factors (e.g., flux surface area, volume derivatives, magnitude of the magnetic field) that are derived from the magnetic configuration ψ(R,Z). Therefore, at each cycle of the transport simulation, the new equilibrium solution ψ must be calculated by the GS solver, and the geometric factors must be continuously updated for the subsequent transport step.[19]

The full predictive capability of integrated modeling relies fundamentally on linking the highly localized external actuator source terms to the dynamic 2D magnetic geometry. Auxiliary systems like ECH or NBI heating and current drive have highly localized deposition profiles that are acutely sensitive to the magnetic field topology and flux surface locations. If the equilibrium shifts—for example, due to an increasing Shafranov shift during the current ramp-up phase—the calculated source term deposition profile (e.g., SE​ or SJ​) must be instantly recalculated to reflect the new geometry. The robust coupling architecture ensures that the calculated 1D profile evolution instantaneously drives a 2D geometry update, which in turn feeds back into the detailed 3D deposition calculation for the next time step, guaranteeing physical consistency for predictive modeling of control maneuvers.

B. Enabling Advanced Control Strategies

The capability for fast, physics-anchored simulation unlocks powerful advanced control strategies:

**1. Control During Dynamic Phases:** The ability to perform real-time 2D equilibrium reconstruction is essential because the plasma configuration changes rapidly during the crucial start-up phase, involving significant magnetic shaping and Shafranov shift.[7] Without this capability, modeling is typically only accurate during the flat-top phase. Integrating fast 2D equilibrium updates allows for closed-loop profile control during the challenging start-up phase, which drastically improves shot reproducibility and efficiency, potentially enabling access to new plasma regimes.[7]

**2. Model Predictive Control (MPC):** MPC is a highly promising technique for profile control. Because the integrated transport code is inherently predictive, it can simulate the plasma's future evolution over a defined time horizon. The MPC algorithm uses this predictive capability to calculate and implement optimal actuator trajectories that minimize the predicted deviation from the desired target state.[7]

**3. State Feedback Techniques:** The constant availability of a high-resolution, estimated state vector x^ facilitates the implementation of sophisticated state feedback control methods, such as Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) or H-infinity control, provided accurate local linearizations of the plasma dynamics can be obtained.[7]

C. Scenario Optimization and Pulse Design

For experiment design (often called open-loop profile control), fast simulation speed is equally valuable. Optimization of actuator trajectories (e.g., current ramp waveforms, heating power sequence) before an experiment begins is critical for devices like ITER.[7] Current design often relies on extensive experience, leading to inefficiency (trial and error).

The low computation times achieved by accelerated methods (like those used in codes such as RAPTOR, leveraging QLKNN) make it possible to use powerful non-linear optimization techniques.[7] These algorithms often require hundreds or thousands of iterations, making standard, computationally heavy physics codes unsuited for the task. The increased speed, coupled with the improved accuracy provided by the 2D equilibrium reconstruction (particularly relevant during the fast-changing start-up phase), provides a good quantitative method for experiment design.[7]

D. Steady-State and Advanced Tokamak Operation Analysis

Integrated modeling is mandatory for designing and analyzing Advanced Tokamak (AT) scenarios, which rely on internal current drive mechanisms, primarily the self-generated bootstrap current (Jbs​), to sustain the plasma current for long periods. These high-performance regimes often require specific plasma shapes, such as high elongation, which tend to result in inherently unstable equilibria that require constant feedback control to sustain.[20]

For steady-state analysis, the objective is to find stationary solutions to the coupled equations where all time derivatives are identically zero (d/dt≡0). This involves iteratively searching for a solution where the integrated transport fluxes exactly balance the calculated energy, particle, and current sources throughout the plasma volume.[16]

The goal of achieving simulation speeds necessary for real-time control serves a deeper purpose than just faster post-shot analysis; it is essential for enabling active stabilization and sustainment of these desired, yet inherently unstable, high-performance operating points. The fusion of accelerated GS and transport models directly addresses the challenge of stabilizing highly elongated equilibria necessary for optimal performance.[20]

VII. Conclusion and Future Directions

The predictive simulation of tokamak plasma dynamics is fundamentally dependent on the mandatory, iterative coupling of the elliptic Grad-Shafranov (GS) equation (defining equilibrium) and the parabolic transport equations (defining profile evolution) via the Current Diffusion Equation (CDE). The ability to accurately and rapidly solve this coupled system—especially for the free-boundary problem—is the core enabling technology for advanced tokamak research and operation.

Achieving the required speed and stability necessitates a sophisticated, hybrid numerical architecture. Robustness against the extreme stiffness of the transport equations is ensured by adopting high-order, Fully Implicit time integration schemes, often solved via Newton-Krylov methods.[9, 10] Efficiency is significantly boosted by specialized convergence accelerators like Mode Multigrid [15], and crucially, by deploying Neural Network emulators (like QLKNN) to perform the physics closure (turbulent flux calculation) at speeds six orders of magnitude faster than the original first-principles codes.[17]

This integration allows the modeling complex physics, including multi-component density evolution and comprehensive source modeling, ensuring that the critical feedback loop between the 1D profile evolution and the resulting 2D equilibrium geometry is correctly handled during dynamic phases.[7]

The ultimate application of these fast, coupled solvers is in active control. By acting as a real-time plasma state observer, the integrated code enables sophisticated state feedback and Model Predictive Control strategies, critical for stabilizing high-performance, elongated, and inherently unstable equilibria.[7, 20]

Future developments will focus on extending these capabilities to address localized physics, such as edge localized modes (ELMs) and impurity transport in the scrape-off layer, and achieving fully autonomous, real-time control systems capable of managing entire operational scenarios in fusion reactors.
